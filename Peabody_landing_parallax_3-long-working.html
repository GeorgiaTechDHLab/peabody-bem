Elizabeth Palmer Peabody was born in 1804, in a town 25 miles northwest of Boston, Massachusetts. Today, she is most famous for her proximity to more well-known writers of the American Renaissance, such as Henry David Thoreau and Nathaniel Hawthorne. But Peabody had impact in her own right. The bookstore that she ran out of her home in Boston functioned as the de facto salon for the transcendentalist movement. She also edited the transcendentalist magazine, The Dial, for some of its most pivotal years. And like many women of the nineteenth century who didnâ€™t have great career options, Peabody eventually became an educator. In fact, she is credited with starting the first kindergarten in the United States.

But what bearing does Peabody and her personal history have on the development of data visualization today?

The answer is both nothing and everything.
